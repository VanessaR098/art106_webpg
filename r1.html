Summary

In Chapter 1, Clark discusses about the various definitions of the term cyborg and the component that make it up. He believes it is not the merging of flesh and wires that define it but rather the transformational potential of the subject. He begins with introducing our relationship with technologies and the seamless qualities it entails regardless of it not being embedded into ourselves. Clark believes that we are natural cyborgs and that we live in an illusion that our mind is separate from the rest of the world. He counteracts this by explaining his analysis of the seamless connections we have with our tools/devices.

Question 1

Clynes and Kline's traditional definition of the term “cyborg” were actually in opposition to new developments causing humans to become something beyond human. Rather, they believed the term “cyborg” and to be a “cyborg” means to allow bodily enhancement but still keeping the mind intact to make its own choices. They believed the human should still be the vessel of these transformations. In comparison, Clark believes all humans are cyborgs in a natural form and don’t require technology to be associated as such.

Summary

In Chapter 2, he begins to delve more deeply what type of technologies we do have a relationship with. Here he separates it as transparents vs opaque technologies and that this distinction is not always fixed and is rather dependent on the user’s versatility towards the tool. He defines opaque technologies to be not naturally integrated, but easily distinguishable from the user while transparent technologies are well fitted with our natural capabilities. He analyzes how naturally we've become accustomed to our tools, such as a watch, dictionary, etc. that he mentions and how it’s shaped our cognitive processes as its user.
